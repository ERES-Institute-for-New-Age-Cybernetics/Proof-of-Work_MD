# **1\. Introduction**

## **1.1 Problem Statement**

All operational propulsion systems require expulsion of reaction mass. The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation imposes exponential mass penalties for high-Δv missions. A propulsion mechanism that generates thrust without expelling onboard mass would fundamentally alter the economics and feasibility of deep-space exploration. The Dynamical Casimir Effect (DCE)—in which accelerating boundaries convert quantum vacuum fluctuations into real photon pairs—offers a candidate mechanism, though one that has historically produced forces far below useful thresholds.

## **1.2 State of the Art**

The static Casimir force between parallel plates was predicted by Casimir (1948) and experimentally confirmed by Lamoreaux (1997) to \~5% accuracy, with subsequent measurements achieving \~1% agreement with theory at 0.1–0.7 μm separations (Klimchitskaya et al. 2024). The Dynamical Casimir Effect was experimentally confirmed by Wilson et al. (2011) using a superconducting circuit, producing real photons from vacuum via effective mirror velocity \~5% of c.

Maclay and Forward (2004) proposed DCE-based propulsion but noted that material strength limits constrain mechanical mirror amplitudes to the nanometer range, producing negligible forces. The critical recent advance is Hyland (2025), who proposes replacing mechanical mirrors with an epitaxial stack of transparent semiconductor laminae in which voltage is rapidly switched to successive layers, creating continuous large-amplitude reflective-surface motion without mechanical contrivances. This architecture achieves centimeter-level effective amplitudes, raising projected propulsive forces by orders of magnitude.

NASA’s Breakthrough Propulsion Physics program (1996–2002) and subsequent DIA assessments have explored Casimir-based propulsion as a long-term research direction, establishing institutional precedent for this line of inquiry.

# **2\. Body of Evidence**

## **2.1 Physics Foundation**

### **2.1.1 Static Casimir Force**

The Casimir force between two parallel perfectly conducting plates separated by distance d is:

**F/A \= \-π²ħc / (240 d⁴)**

For d \= 100 nm, this yields approximately 1.3 × 10⁻³ N/m² (\~130 μN/cm²). The force has been measured to \~1% precision in plate-sphere geometries using atomic force microscopy and torsion-balance techniques (Decca et al. 2009; Mohideen et al. 2024). This is established physics.

### **2.1.2 Dynamical Casimir Effect (DCE)**

When a boundary moves at velocity v in vacuum, it converts virtual photons into real photon pairs. The photon production rate scales as (v/c)². For mechanical mirrors with nanometer amplitudes at GHz frequencies, v/c \~ 10⁻¹⁶, producing unmeasurably small photon fluxes. Wilson et al. (2011) circumvented this by using a superconducting quantum interference device (SQUID) to modulate an effective boundary condition at \~10 GHz, achieving v\_eff/c \~ 0.05 and observing real photon production.

The DCE is now experimentally confirmed. The open question is whether the photon momentum can be made directionally asymmetric to produce net thrust.

### **2.1.3 Hyland Epitaxial Stack Architecture (2025)**

Hyland’s key innovation replaces mechanical mirror motion with sequential voltage switching across an epitaxial stack of transparent semiconductor laminae. As voltage is applied to each lamina in sequence, a reflective surface effectively propagates through the stack at a speed determined by switching rate, not mechanical inertia. This permits effective boundary velocities approaching relativistic fractions at centimeter-level amplitudes, without material stress. Hyland provides relativistic force calculations showing that at centimeter-scale amplitudes, propulsive forces rise to potentially measurable levels—orders of magnitude above the nanometer-amplitude mechanical limit identified by Maclay and Forward.

This architecture is the empirical anchor for ESVRD v4.0.

### **2.1.4 Asymmetric Cavity Geometry**

Directional thrust requires breaking the symmetry of photon emission. Two established approaches exist: truncated-cone (asymmetric frustum) cavities that preferentially emit DCE photons in one direction due to unequal boundary conditions; and layered refractive-mirror systems (explored by the NASA BPP program) where refraction forces and reflection forces are combined to produce net momentum transfer. ESVRD v4.0 proposes combining the Hyland epitaxial stack with asymmetric frustum geometry.

## **2.2 Resonance Amplification**

### **2.2.1 Parametric Resonance in Cavities**

Law (1994) predicted that when the mechanical oscillation frequency of a cavity boundary equals an odd-integer multiple of the fundamental optical resonance frequency, the rate of DCE photon emission increases dramatically—a parametric resonance effect. This is the mechanism by which a small input (boundary oscillation energy) can produce amplified output (photon flux), analogous to SYU’s megaphone analogy: the cavity provides structural resonance that amplifies a small initial input.

This parametric amplification is standard quantum optics. The ESVRD framework organizes it under the resonance condition:

**f\_drive \= (2n+1) × f\_cavity    (n \= 0, 1, 2, ...)**

When this condition is met with sufficient quality factor Q and coupling strength C \> C\_min, photon production rates increase by factors proportional to Q² (for high-Q cavities).

### **2.2.2 Lorentzian Response Profile**

The thrust response as a function of frequency detuning follows a standard Lorentzian:

**T(Δf) \= T\_peak / \[1 \+ (Δf / γ)²\]**

where Δf \= f\_drive − f\_resonance, and γ is the cavity linewidth (determined by Q-factor: γ \= f\_resonance / Q). This is identical to any driven resonator’s amplitude response—established physics, not a novel claim. The practical implication is that thrust is highly sensitive to frequency alignment: detuning by one linewidth reduces thrust by 50%.

## **2.5 Falsifiability Pathway**

### **2.5.1 Phase 1 Experiment**

**Objective:** Detect directional force from a DCE cavity with asymmetric geometry, using a torsion balance, under controlled conditions.

**Apparatus:** Hyland-type epitaxial stack mounted inside asymmetric frustum cavity, suspended on torsion balance inside vacuum chamber (\< 10⁻⁶ torr).

**Drive:** Sequential voltage switching at frequency f\_drive \= f\_cavity (fundamental parametric resonance).

**Measurement:** Torsion-balance deflection via optical lever, sensitivity \~1 nN.

**Controls:** Symmetric cavity (same stack, cylindrical geometry) to verify null result; off-resonance drive (f\_drive ≠ f\_cavity) to verify frequency dependence; stack powered but static (no switching) to verify DCE origin.

**Success criterion:** Repeatable directional force \> 3σ above noise floor, with correct frequency dependence (Lorentzian profile), and null result in all three controls. Independent replication by at least one external laboratory.

**Failure criterion:** No measurable force above noise floor across the full predicted frequency range after \>100 hours of integrated measurement time; OR force detected but present in symmetric-cavity control (indicating non-DCE origin); OR force present but no frequency dependence (indicating thermal/electromagnetic artifact).

### **2.5.2 Phase 1b: Coherence-Coupling Validation**

**Objective:** Determine whether operator HRV coherence state produces measurable differences in cavity tuning precision and, indirectly, in force output.

**Protocol:** Run Phase 1 experiment under three conditions: (a) automated tuning only, (b) BEST-coupled tuning (operator in high-coherence state, HRV \> 0.7), (c) BEST-coupled tuning (operator in low-coherence state, HRV \< 0.3, achieved via stress induction protocol with informed consent).

**Success criterion:** Statistically significant difference (p \< 0.05) in tuning precision or force stability between conditions (b) and (c), after controlling for automated-only baseline (a).

**Failure criterion:** No significant difference between conditions. This would indicate that operator-state coupling adds no value to cavity control, and the BEST feedback channel should be removed from subsequent versions.

* The asymmetric DCE cavity may not produce net directional force due to momentum conservation in the photon field (the cavity frame may recoil without net external thrust). This is the central open question.

* Hyland’s epitaxial stack may not achieve predicted effective velocities due to switching-speed limitations in real semiconductors.

* Thermal photon background may overwhelm DCE photon signal at laboratory temperatures.
