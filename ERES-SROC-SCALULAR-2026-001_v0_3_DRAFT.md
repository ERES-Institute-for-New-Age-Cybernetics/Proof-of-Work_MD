## §1. Contact of Record — the Opening Requirement

**A contract begins with a number anyone can call.**

An instrument that names no reachable party is a publication, not a contract. Reachability is the operational form of answerability: if no one can be reached, no one is answerable, and if no one is answerable there is no Patron — and without a Patron the IDEA cascade does not close at any band, S5 included.

Every SROC MUST therefore open with a Contact of Record stating:

1. A working voice channel, published, answered by a person.
2. The name of the party answerable for the contract's operation.
3. The hours and language(s) of response, and the maximum response interval.
4. The escalation path when the interval is missed.
5. The date from which the channel has been continuously available.

**Rules.** The Contact of Record is not severable — an SROC with a dead channel is void, not merely defective. It may not be a form, a portal, an address, or an automated agent standing alone. Change of channel requires notice under §5 and does not reset the §1.5 continuity date.

> **Note on the numeral.** The author associates this requirement with 216. The **requirement** — a published number anyone can call — is load-bearing. The **numeral** is symbolic and is not derived by this document; it becomes operative only if and when it denotes an actually reachable channel.

### §1.6 Custody of Answerability and the Erasure Boundary (added v0.2)

**The problem.** §1 makes the Contact of Record non-severable. But answerability is not a present-tense fact — it is a historical one. To hold a party to a breach, the record must show *who was answerable, and when*. That record is a chain of custody. An Erasure Clause permits a party to demand removal of data. If the answerable party can erase the record of their own answerability, §1 is void by construction, and precisely at the moment it is needed.

**The corpus already resolves this asymmetrically, and correctly.** Erasure in ERES is asymptotic, not binary: CBGMODD audits completeness and reports an **Erasure Completeness Ratio (ECR ∈ [0, 1); the value 1.0 is refused as a binary certificate)** rather than issuing a binary certificate (ERES-ECR-2026-001; FAVORS Layer 7 Deletion). ECR §6.7 records air-gapped retention as having no defense *by construction*, and the instrument's legal-evidence provisions already require ECR to be paired with traditional chain-of-custody attestations in evidentiary settings — ECR is one instrument in the record, not the record.

**Rule.** In any SROC:

1. **Personal and operational data is erasable** to the ECR limit, on the terms of FAVORS Layer 7 and the withdrawal rights of the Visible Chain of Custody.
2. **The custody record of answerability is not erasable.** The attestation of which party held the Contact of Record, over which interval, survives every erasure request, including that party's own.
3. **The two are distinguishable by construction:** the answerability record contains party, role, interval, and channel state — not the subject data the contract processed.
4. An SROC that permits (2) to be erased is void under §1, on the same ground as a dead channel.

**Why this is consistent rather than an exception.** The ECR residual — the unenumerable surface that erasure provably cannot reach — is the same surface on which custody attestation rests. The corpus does not claim erasure is total; it measures how near total it gets. Custody of answerability is the part of the residual that is retained *deliberately and disclosed*, rather than retained *unavoidably and reported*.

**Downstream (revised v0.3).** The Mind–Body–SPRT triad — the three coupled answerability surfaces a Patron must hold at any band — and any BUILD ORDER issued at s / S / $ tier inherit this boundary: a BUILD ORDER inherits the answerability custody of the SROC that issued it, and cannot be erased into anonymity by its issuer. (The triad is named here for downstream reference; its own operational definition sits upstream in the corpus and is not restated by this document.)

## §2. What an SROC Is (CANDIDATE definition)

A Smart Resonant Offset Contract is proposed as a fiduciary instrument that:

- **Smart** — executes stated allocation rules without discretionary intervention at execution time;
- **Resonant** — its rules are indexed to a measured band rather than to fixed nominal values;
- **Offset** — it discharges obligation by transfer against a measured deficit, rather than by accrual to a holder;
- **Contract** — it binds a named, reachable, answerable party under §1.

This definition is CANDIDATE. It has not been circulated, rated, or ratified.

## §3. The Problem Path (e) Addresses

At S5 the Declaration requires a Patron: a fiduciary counterparty who accepts delegation at planetary band. No planetary body exists that can accept. The single "GAIA Actuary Steward" was proposed to fill that seat, and two MIEVM rounds found it in tension with the Declaration's own broad-distributable-patronage requirement.

Path (e) relocates the Patron from a **body** to an **instrument**. A contract need not exist as an institution; it executes. This dissolves the "no planetary body exists" objection and satisfies broad-distributability natively, since a contract's counterparties are many by construction.

## §5. The Binding Open Question — Amendment Authority

A contract executes; it does not accept. Acceptance in the Declaration's sense means bearing duty and being answerable for breach. An SROC allocates according to rules; it cannot itself be answerable.

Therefore: **whoever holds authority to deploy, amend, pause, or key the SROC is the actual S5 Patron**, and the broad-distributable-patronage requirement applies to *that* party, not to the contract.

Three dispositions are possible, and this document selects none:

| Disposition | Consequence for S5 |
|---|---|
| Amendment authority held by one party | Path (e) collapses into the single-Steward form; the original tension returns unchanged |
| Amendment authority held by a defined group | Path (e) collapses into path (a) or (c); assess under those |
| Amendment authority genuinely distributed across participants | Path (e) is distinct — **but distribution of answerability must be demonstrated, not asserted** |

**This is the open question on which path (e) stands or falls.** It is unanswered at v0.3.
