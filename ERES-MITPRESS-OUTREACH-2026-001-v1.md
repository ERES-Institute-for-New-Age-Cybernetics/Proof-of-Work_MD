## Section I — MIT Press Contact Protocol (Verified)

The MIT Press does not accept full proposals or manuscripts on first contact. The verified procedure is:

1. Send a **brief inquiry** (a few paragraphs) by email or post to the appropriate acquisitions editor.
2. The inquiry should describe the project, the author, and the reasons for writing the book.
3. Do **not** attach manuscripts, sample chapters, or files unless specifically invited.
4. If interested, the editor will request a full proposal and sample chapters.

**AI disclosure.** MIT Press policy (current as of 2026) requires that any substantive use of AI tools be transparently disclosed to the editor and in the manuscript itself. AI systems cannot be listed as authors. The MIEVM methodology must be disclosed in the inquiry letter — disclosed cleanly, it strengthens rather than weakens the submission, because the methodology is author-designed and the author retains full responsibility for the work.

## Section VII — Follow-Up Timing and Etiquette

- **Send Letter 1 (Kehoe)** as the opening move. Email is preferable to post; subject line as drafted.
- **Wait 4–6 weeks before any follow-up.** University press editors carry heavy submission queues; faster follow-up reads as pressure.
- **If no response by week 6:** send a single brief follow-up to Kehoe (one paragraph: "Following up on my April [date] inquiry — would welcome a brief indication of fit or redirection"). One follow-up only; do not stack.
- **If no response to follow-up by week 10:** send Letter 2 to Bueno Gibbs. Do **not** mention the prior outreach to Kehoe — let each inquiry stand on its own footing.
- **If Letter 2 likewise produces no traction by week 16:** send Letter 3 to Woods, again as a fresh inquiry.
- **Escalation backstop:** if all three sequential approaches yield silence, the Editorial Director (Janice Audet) is the appropriate final contact, framed as a brief note asking for guidance on whether the Press is the right home and, if so, who is best positioned to evaluate the project.

The full sequence, run with discipline, is approximately a four-month window. That pace matches MIT Press's own review tempo and protects the project's standing rather than spending it.
