# **3\. Problem — Identity & Attestation in Australia**

### **3.1 Card-not-present fraud is the dominant payment-fraud vector**

AusPayNet's 2025 Australian Payment Fraud Report is unambiguous. Total payment-card fraud on Australian-issued cards reached A$913M in calendar 2024, a 20% increase year-over-year. CNP fraud alone was A$816M (+19%), of which overseas CNP grew 25% to A$454M and domestic CNP grew 11% to A$362M. CNP now represents roughly 85% of all card fraud in Australia. The fraud rate climbed from 70.2 cents per A$1,000 spent in 2023 to 78.8 cents in 2024\.

FY25 (12 months to 30 June 2025\) showed a modest decline to A$854M. The trend, however, is structural: fraud migrates to channels where the human is hardest to verify, and CNP is by definition the channel where the human is not present.

### **3.2 Voice and biometric deepfakes are accelerating the threat**

Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) under the AusPayNet CNP Fraud Mitigation Framework currently relies primarily on knowledge factors and possession factors (OTP, push-confirmation). Both are now spoofable at scale by readily-available generative-AI tooling. The deepfake-voice attack surface is no longer hypothetical — banks are seeing it in incident logs in 2025–2026. Static biometric templates do not help: they are stealable, and once stolen they are unrenewable.

### **3.3 Identity sprawl across civic services**

A single Australian resident interacts with water, power, housing assistance, Medicare, ATO, Services Australia, state transport, and local council services through a fragmented credential ecosystem. Mobile driver's licences in NSW, VIC, and QLD address part of the problem; myID addresses another part for federal services; ConnectID and other private providers (IDVerse, Makesure Consulting — applications pending with ACCC as of mid-2026) will address yet other parts once Phase 3–4 of AGDIS opens. Even with all of these working as designed, there is no continuous attestation layer that ties a specific civic action to a specific living human authorisation.

### **3.4 The private digital-ID market is fragile**

Australia Post shut down its Digital iD product on 30 April 2026, after nine years of operation, citing the changed landscape created by mobile driver's licences and myID. Independent commentary has characterised this as a market failure analogous to the UK's failed private digital-ID providers. The structural barrier was the exclusion of private providers from AGDIS until December 2026, which limited the commercial pathways available to build sustainable user bases. Department of Finance has indicated only 5–10 private-sector providers are expected to participate in AGDIS, with more possibly joining later.

This is the gap. The market needs a new posture: not another identity-issuance app competing for end-user mindshare, but an attestation and integrity layer that any accredited identity provider — and any relying party — can plug into. That is what EAAP is.

### **3.5 The fundamental claim**

*You cannot prove, beyond reasonable doubt, that a specific human authorised a specific transaction at a specific moment.*

Every painful consequence downstream — fraud disputes with no definitive proof, contracts signed without continuous consent, civic remediation that defaults to punishment because there is no audit trail — flows from this single unfilled requirement.

# **4\. Solution — EAAP**

### **4.1 What EAAP Is**

EAAP — the ERES Attestation and Authorization Protocol — is a byte-normative cryptographic standard that captures, signs, and time-stamps a live bio-electric or voice signature event at the moment a human authorises an action, and produces a non-repudiable receipt that downstream systems (and courts) can verify.

EAAP is not a blockchain, not a password manager, not a mass-surveillance tool, and not a competitor to myID. It is an attestation layer that runs alongside whatever identity provider, directory service, or payment rail is already in place.

### **4.2 The three-step user experience**

| Step | What Happens | Human Experience |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1\. Intent | User indicates they want to approve a payment, login, document, or service access. | Tap, click, or short voice command. |
| 2\. Biometric Signature | EAAP captures a live bio-electric or voice sample. Raw biometric data is never stored — only a cryptographic envelope keyed to the live event. | Speak a short phrase or touch a sensor. |
| 3\. Attestation | Protocol produces a signed, time-stamped, byte-normative receipt with hardware-rooted sensor attestation (TPM/TEE). | Sub-second. The user does nothing further. |

### **4.3 What is preserved**

A court-admissible, auditable proof that this person approved this action at this time. The receipt survives indefinitely. The biometric event itself does not need to be retained as raw data; the cryptographic envelope is sufficient for verification.

### **4.4 The canonical interlock**

Within ERES the attestation is expressed as the canonical interlock formula:

*(CBGMODD × FAVORS) \+ BERA \= RATE*

CBGMODD provides the governance and consent context, FAVORS supplies the relational and attribute layer, BERA contributes the live measurement (ARI / ERI / RHC / RCI), and the resulting RATE — semantically unresolvable except through the matched key — is named VEILED. For investor purposes the takeaway is that the receipt cannot be reconstructed from any subset of its inputs; it requires the live human moment to resolve.

# **5\. Product & Technology**

### **5.1 Architectural overview**

EAAP v1.2 is a 2,885-line byte-normative specification covering protocol semantics, test vectors, hardware attestation requirements, and post-quantum migration. The protocol is deployable in three reference configurations: cloud-hosted, on-premises, and air-gapped. All three produce identical receipts; the differences are in where the attestation server lives and how key custody is handled.

### **5.2 The three polysemantic primitives**

EAAP rests on the ERES Cryptographic Stack Primitive (v1.3), which formalises three interlocking constructs:

* GCF — the governance/consent framing primitive.

* EDF — the Entity Data Framework, supplying attribute and relational context.

* USC — the unified signature construct, binding the live biometric event to the receipt.

These three primitives compose into the canonical interlock and are the source of EAAP's resistance to replay, substitution, and template-theft attacks.

### **5.3 Determinism, hardware roots, and the post-quantum path**

* Integer cross-multiplication replaces floating-point arithmetic throughout the protocol, eliminating IEEE-754 non-determinism. Every node computes the same receipt for the same inputs, byte for byte.

* Sensor attestation is rooted in TPM or TEE hardware. The attestation server can verify that the biometric event came from a sensor it trusts, not a synthesised replay.

* Post-quantum migration to ML-DSA and Falcon is scheduled across 2026–2032, with the protocol designed to support hybrid signatures during the transition window. Enterprise customers receive a documented migration plan, not a marketing promise.

### **5.4 The MIEVM validation methodology**

Every architecturally significant change to EAAP — and to every other ERES protocol — passes through the Multi-Instrument Ensemble Validation Method. Four independent large-language-model nodes (Claude, DeepSeek, Grok, ChatGPT) review the change in parallel; the convergent mean rating across rigorously specified dimensions must reach 9.0+ before the change is accepted into canon. EAAP v1.2 reached convergent mean 9.4 across its four-pass validation in April 2026; the Berggruen Prize Essay reached 9.8.

MIEVM is documented as a publishable research methodology in its own right.
