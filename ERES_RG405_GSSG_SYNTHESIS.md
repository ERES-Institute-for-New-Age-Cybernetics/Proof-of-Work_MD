# **I. Prior Art and Peer-Reviewed Foundation**

The GSSG synthesis protocol is grounded in published, peer-reviewed research demonstrating graphene production from sugar on sand/silica substrates:

1. **Graphene Sand Composite (GSC):** Researchers demonstrated synthesis of graphenic material from cane sugar on natural sand by pyrolysis at 750°C under N₂ atmosphere. Raman spectroscopy confirmed graphenic character with G-band at 1597 cm⁻¹ and D-band at 1338 cm⁻¹. The SiO₂ peak at 470 cm⁻¹ confirmed substrate presence. Published on ResearchGate: “Graphene from Sugar and its Application in Water Purification.”

2. **SiO₂@Carbon-Graphene Hybrids:** Colloidal silica \+ sucrose composites prepared via hydrothermal and heat treatment. Demonstrated 542 mAh/g capacity at the 216th cycle (the ordinal gematria of Joseph Allen Sprute). Published in Journal of Energy Chemistry.

3. **Sucrose as Carbon Source (Rice University/Nature):** Single-layer graphene grown from sucrose on copper substrate at 800°C. Topological defects self-healed during formation. Confirmed: graphene from sugar self-heals.

4. **Biomass Pyrolysis Review:** Comprehensive review confirming glucose, sucrose, and biomass waste as viable graphene precursors via pyrolysis under inert atmosphere. Published in Chemical Engineering Journal (2020).

The ERES contribution is not the chemistry—which is established—but the architectural integration: GSSG as the material platform for self-healing, self-reporting structures that function as Gas Vapor Detectors for the built environment.

* **Raman spectrometer:** 532nm or 633nm laser, 100–3000 cm⁻¹ range. For validation.

# **IV. Synthesis Procedure**

## **IV.A Substrate Preparation**

5. Wash natural sand with deionized water (3 cycles) to remove organic contaminants and fine particulates.

6. Dry at 110°C for 2 hours. Weigh precisely.

7. Weigh sugar at 1:2 ratio to sand (e.g., 25g sugar per 50g sand for 2:1 sand:sugar).

8. Mix sugar into sand thoroughly. For uniform coating, dissolve sugar in minimum deionized water, add sand, stir to coat, then pre-dry at 80°C for 1 hour.

## **IV.B Pyrolysis**

**Temperature ramp protocol** (adapted from peer-reviewed GSC procedure):

| Step | Temperature | Duration | Rate | Purpose |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | RT → 100°C | 30 min | \~2.5°C/min | Moisture removal |
| 2 | 100°C → 200°C | 30 min | \~3.3°C/min | Sugar melting |
| 3 | Hold 200°C | 60 min | Isothermal | Uniform coating |
| 4 | 200°C → 750°C | 60 min | \~9.2°C/min | Carbonization |
| 5 | Hold 750°C | 180 min (3 hr) | Isothermal | Graphitization |
| 6 | 750°C → RT | Natural cooling | Uncontrolled | Cooling |

**Atmosphere:** N₂ flow at 150 sccm throughout Steps 1–6. Begin N₂ purge 15 minutes before Step 1 to displace oxygen.

**Critical control:** Oxygen exclusion. Any O₂ presence during Steps 4–5 will combust the carbon rather than graphitize it. Verify furnace seal and N₂ flow integrity before each run.

## **IV.C Post-Processing (Optional Activation)**

9. Cool product to room temperature under N₂.

10. For surface activation: treat 5g of product with 10mL concentrated H₂SO₄, stir 30 min, wash with deionized water until pH neutral, dry at 110°C. (Enhances adsorptive and piezoresistive properties.)

11. Store in sealed container under desiccant.

# **V. Validation Criteria**

## **V.A Raman Spectroscopy**

**Required peaks for graphenic confirmation:**

| Peak | Position (cm⁻¹) | Assignment | Acceptance Criterion |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| D-band | \~1338–1350 | Defects/edges in sp² carbon | Present, sharp |
| G-band | \~1580–1600 | sp² carbon stretching (graphene) | Present, sharp, I\_G \> I\_D |
| 2D-band | \~2650–2700 | Graphene layer stacking | Present \= few-layer graphene |
| SiO₂ peak | \~465–470 | Quartz substrate | Present \= substrate integrated |

**I\_G/I\_D ratio \> 1.0:** Confirms graphenic character dominates over amorphous carbon. Higher ratio \= higher quality graphene.

## **V.B Electrical Conductivity**

Measure sheet resistance using 4-point probe method. Graphenic GSSG should show conductivity 2–3 orders of magnitude higher than untreated sand. Target: \< 100 Ω/sq for activated GSSG.

## **V.C Piezoresistive Response**

Apply controlled compressive load (0–100 N) to GSSG sample while monitoring resistance. Graphenic GSSG should show measurable ΔR/R₀ proportional to applied strain. This validates the self-reporting property described in RG\#403: the material announces its own stress state, functioning as a structural VAPOR detector.

# **VI. Sugar-Carbon Reservoir for Self-Healing**

The Sugar-Carbon Reservoir Hypothesis (ERES-GSSG-SH-WP-2026-001) proposes that distributing excess sugar-derived carbon throughout the GSSG matrix creates a latent repair substrate. When localized stress produces micro-cracks, heat at the crack site can re-mobilize nearby amorphous carbon, which migrates to the defect and reforms graphenic lattice.

**Protocol for carbon reservoir loading:**

12. Increase sugar ratio to 1:1 (sand:sugar) instead of 2:1. This produces excess amorphous carbon interspersed with graphenic regions.

13. During Step 5 (750°C hold), reduce duration to 90 minutes instead of 180\. This produces partial graphitization: graphenic domains surrounded by amorphous carbon reservoirs.

14. Validate by Raman: I\_G/I\_D ratio should be 0.6–1.0 (mixed graphenic/amorphous), indicating both crystalline graphene and available carbon reservoir.

Self-healing test: introduce controlled scratch (Vickers indenter, 1N), heat locally (500°C, 30min, N₂), re-measure Raman and conductivity at scratch site. Recovery of G-band intensity and conductivity \= successful self-healing event.
