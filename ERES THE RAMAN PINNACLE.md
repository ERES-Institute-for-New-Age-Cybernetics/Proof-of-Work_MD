### **1.2 The Thesis of This Paper**

This paper argues that the missing measurement is not complex, expensive, or institutionally inaccessible. It is a single Raman spectrum from a simple materials science protocol: table sugar pyrolyzed on washed sand at 750°C under nitrogen atmosphere. If the G-band peak at \~1597 cm⁻¹ is present, GSSG (Green Solar-Sand Glass with Infused Graphene) is confirmed by standard analytical chemistry. If the peak is absent, the protocol fails falsifiably and must be revised.

More importantly, the presence or absence of this peak changes the game-theoretic landscape for every player who has an interest in the ERES claims. Before the peak, the dominant strategy is to ignore. After the peak, the dominant strategy shifts to engage—because ignoring a falsifiable, reproducible, independently verifiable material claim is no longer costless.

### **1.3 The Dual Meaning of "Pinnacle"**

The word *pinnacle* carries two meanings simultaneously:

* Spectral pinnacle: The G-band peak at \~1597 cm⁻¹ on a Raman spectrum. A sharp, narrow emission where scattered photons resonate with the sp² carbon-carbon stretching mode of graphene. Present: the material is graphenic. Absent: the material is amorphous carbon or unconverted sugar char.

This paper asserts that these two pinnacles are the same point. The spectral peak *is* the strategic peak. One measurement changes every strategy.

### **4.3 The Raman Tradition in Carbon Science**

Raman spectroscopy has been the gold standard for graphene characterization since its isolation in 2004 (Novoselov, Geim, et al., Nobel Prize 2010). The G-band (\~1580 cm⁻¹) corresponds to the E₂g phonon mode of sp² carbon atoms. The D-band (\~1350 cm⁻¹) is activated by defects and edges. The 2D-band (\~2700 cm⁻¹) is the second order of the D-band and is sensitive to layer stacking. The I\_G/I\_D ratio \> 1.0 indicates that graphenic character dominates over amorphous carbon. These criteria are not ERES inventions—they are standard analytical chemistry. RG\#407 simply points to them and says: *use these.*
