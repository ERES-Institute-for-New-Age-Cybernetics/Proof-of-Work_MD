This summary is for first-time readers — policymakers, regulators, judges, journalists, and citizens. The full legal argument follows from §1.

## **2.5 The Legal Paramountcy Claim, Formalized**

A legal claim is paramount when it satisfies three conditions: logical priority (the claim must be true for any other claim in the domain to be meaningfully evaluated), normative weight (violation of the claim outweighs competing considerations subject to enumerated limits), and necessary precondition (without it, the legal system cannot deliver its promised protections within the relevant domain).

### **Application to ECR**

* **Logical priority:** a law that requires erasure cannot be meaningfully enforced unless erasure completeness is measurable. Expungement, GDPR Art. 17, HIPAA disposal, debt satisfaction — all are operationally void without a falsifiable completeness metric.

* **Normative weight:** the harm from unverifiable residuals (indefinite penalty, contaminated credit, sovereign bias, generational unfairness) outweighs the cost of implementing ECR, subject to the competing-interests analysis in §2.6.

* **Necessary precondition:** no existing regime provides an alternative falsifiable erasure metric. ECR is the only specified candidate at the time of writing. If a peer instrument emerges with equal or greater rigor, the paramountcy claim transfers to the better instrument — it is the property of falsifiable erasure, not the property of ECR-as-such.

The paramountcy claim is therefore both substantive (the property is real and necessary) and instrument-defeasible (any superior falsifiable erasure metric inherits the claim). This is consistent with the recursive completeness requirement specified in §3.4.

## **2.6 Competing Interests and Limits of Paramountcy**

v1.1 introduces this section because no genuine legal-paramountcy claim survives without engagement with the interests it might override. The paramountcy of provable erasure is bounded by, and must be reconciled with, the following enumerated interests:

### **Archival, historical, and journalistic interest**

The right to be forgotten has been repeatedly tempered by archival interest (CJEU GC and Others v. CNIL, Case C‑136/17, 2019\) and journalistic privilege (GDPR Art. 17(3)(a)). ECR does not require that all records be erased — it requires that when erasure is mandated, it be measurable. Archival exceptions remain operative; ECR applies to the residue after lawful exception is determined. The petition framework in §3.5 explicitly preserves archival, historical, and journalistic exceptions as enumerated carve-outs.

### **Criminal evidence preservation and law enforcement**

Erasure cannot extinguish evidence preservation duties under Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure 16 disclosure, or analogous foreign duties. ECR governs erasure when erasure is lawful; it does not authorize the destruction of materially exculpatory or required-preservation records. A jurisdiction adopting ECR must specify the law-enforcement preservation hierarchy: where preservation and erasure conflict, preservation prevails subject to ordinary spoliation doctrine, and ECR computes only over the erasure-eligible residue.

### **Freedom of expression and the right to be remembered**

There is an emerging counter-doctrine recognizing a right to be remembered — protecting historical memory of public events, public figures, and individuals’ self-narrated digital legacies from unilateral erasure by third parties. ECR is neutral on this: it measures the completeness of erasure when erasure is the lawful disposition, not the lawfulness of the disposition itself. The petition should require that ECR adoption be paired with explicit guidance on when erasure is the wrong remedy.

### **National security and state secrecy**

Classification regimes (E.O. 13526 in the U.S., analogous regimes abroad) impose preservation and access-control obligations distinct from erasure. A node operating under classification can lawfully refuse propagation-graph disclosure; under §4.7 of the technical document, such a node is treated as a residual leakage contribution to (1 − ECR) for civil-erasure purposes, without compelling declassification. The civil and classified surfaces remain methodologically separate.

### **Property interest of holders of disclosed records**

Where a party other than the data subject has a property or contractual interest in continued retention (e.g., security interest, ongoing litigation hold, regulatory examination file), that interest survives the data subject’s erasure request to the extent the controlling regime preserves it. ECR computes against the lawfully-erasure-eligible population; preserved-interest holders are not non-cooperative nodes in the §4.7 sense.

The paramountcy claim survives these limits intact: in each case ECR is the measurement instrument for the residue of what is lawfully erasable. The competing interests do not refute the claim; they delineate its operational domain.

## **2.7 Rating Agency and Accounting Mechanics**

v1.1 introduces this section to make the sovereign-credit and IFRS / CECL claims operational rather than aspirational.

### **IFRS 9 / CECL integration**

Under IFRS 9 (international) and ASC 326 CECL (U.S.), lifetime expected credit loss models combine probability of default (PD), loss given default (LGD), and exposure at default (EAD). A retired obligation — satisfied, restructured, discharged — that nevertheless persists in calibration data or feature stores biases the historical loss window. The methodological adjustment that ECR enables is enumerated:

1. Obligations with published ECR at or above the SCALULAR LAW pillar floor (≥0.90 for PII-touching data per ERES-ECR-2026-001 v1.3 §4.6) may be excluded from EAD with reference to the publication date.

2. Historical loss windows that include such obligations should be recalibrated to exclude the ECR-retired portion, with the recalibration disclosed in the model documentation under SR 11-7 (U.S.) or analogous supervisory guidance.

3. Models that continue to use ECR-retired data must document the methodological reason and demonstrate that the residual signal is materially predictive on independent test data.

### **Auditor attestation under ISAE 3000 / AT-C 205**

ECR publications are subject to assurance under ISAE 3000 (international) or AT-C section 205 (U.S.). The proposed audit program covers (a) verification of CBGMODD propagation-graph completeness against COI charter, (b) testing of the Horvitz–Thompson stratified-sampling estimator including per-stratum capture-rate estimation, (c) verification of EAAP-CRL post-quantum signature chain under ML-DSA / Falcon, (d) MIEVM ensemble disagreement-bound compliance, and (e) VEILED residency-clock conformance. The auditor’s opinion is itself published and admits independent re-derivation.

### **Rating-agency methodology integration**

Major NRSROs (S\&P Global, Moody’s, Fitch) publish operational-risk and data-governance factors in their sovereign and structured-finance methodologies. ECR adoption integrates with these factors in three ways: first, an issuer’s ECR publication record is itself a data-governance signal; second, the ECR-retired portion of restructured obligations is methodologically excludable from default analysis where the rating agency has discretion under its published methodology; third, persistent VEILED states or sustained Δ divergences are themselves negative signals warranting downgrade pressure. None of this compels rating-agency action; it makes ECR-relevant evidence available to the methodology each agency has already published, which is the lawful mode of influence on private-actor rating decisions.

### **GDPR adequacy and cross-border transfers (Art. 45–49)**

Article 45 adequacy decisions, Art. 46 appropriate safeguards (including Standard Contractual Clauses post-Schrems II), and Art. 49 derogations all require effective protection of erasure rights at the destination.
