**4\. The IDIPITIS Security Model**

v1.3 stated the invariants of the primitive without naming the security model that protects them. v1.4 introduces the IDIPITIS Security Model explicitly, with threat model, invariant pattern, and failure conditions.

### **4.1 The IDIPITIS Principle as Security Invariant**

**IDIPITIS** — "not all knowledge is concurrently disclosed knowledge" — is the named architectural commitment that distinguishes withheld information from absent information from denied information. In v1.3, IDIPITIS was cited as the rationale for the VEILED state. In v1.4, IDIPITIS is upgraded to the role of **security invariant**: the property the primitive's operations must preserve.

### **4.2 The Binary Stride 111000111 / 000111000**

The IDIPITIS invariant is encoded as a nine-bit binary stride alternating between disclosure (1) and veil (0):

| Stride | Pattern | Reading   |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Forward-faith | 111000111 | Three states of disclosure → three states of veil → three states of disclosure. The faithful operational pattern: disclosure earns veil, veil earns disclosure, and the alternation is preserved through three-cycle stewardship. |
| Reverse-faith | 000111000 | Three states of veil → three states of disclosure → three states of veil. The mirror pattern: equally faithful, read from the IDIPITIS invariant's reverse direction. The stride is its own dual. |

The two strides are mirror-reflective: read forward, the pattern alternates 1→0→1; read reversed, it alternates 0→1→0. Both readings encode the same architectural commitment — that disclosure and veil exist in measured alternation, and neither dominates. The three-bit stride length is significant: it composes with Triune Math (three pillars), Trifurcated Math (three branches), and the doubled-three structure of the seven RATE dimensions (R₁–R₃ disclosure-side, R₄ axial, R₅–R₇ veil-side under the mirror reading).

### **4.3 IPIDITIS — The Palindromic Failure Mode**

**IPIDITIS** is the palindromic inverse of IDIPITIS — same letters, mirrored stride. Where IDIPITIS names the principle preserved in faithful operation, IPIDITIS names the corruption: when what should remain veiled is forced into disclosure, or what should be disclosed is improperly veiled. The two terms are EMIT-conformant duality (Energy Marriage Item Time): forward-faith and reverse-corruption read from the same letters.  
In binary stride terms, IPIDITIS-corruption manifests as collapse of the alternating pattern into one of two terminal failure conditions:

| Failure | Pattern | Failure Reading   |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| All-disclosed | 111111111 | Total disclosure. IDIPITIS violated by forced exposure. Privacy collapse, surveillance corruption, the panopticon failure mode. |
| All-veiled | 000000000 | Total opacity. IDIPITIS violated by suppressed disclosure. Information blackout, accountability collapse, the secrecy-state failure mode. |

Both failure modes are equally corrupt. The security model is symmetric: IDIPITIS does not privilege disclosure over veil or veil over disclosure; it requires the alternation, and corruption is collapse to either terminal state. This symmetry is what distinguishes the IDIPITIS Security Model from conventional confidentiality models (which tend to privilege secrecy) and conventional transparency models (which tend to privilege disclosure).

### **4.4 Threat Model**

The threat against the ERES Cryptographic Stack Primitive is not a bit-flipping adversary in the conventional sense. Bit-flipping adversaries are addressed at the protocol layer (EAAP v1.3, slot 432\) through ML-DSA / Falcon post-quantum signatures and integer cross-multiplication determinism. The threat the primitive itself defends against is **disclosure-pattern collapse**: any operation, adversary, or systemic pressure that drives the IDIPITIS stride toward 111111111 or 000000000\.  
The adversary class is therefore broader than conventional cryptographic adversaries:

* **Surveillance adversaries** — pressure toward 111111111\. Defended by the VEILED state's principled non-disclosure.  
* **Suppression adversaries** — pressure toward 000000000\. Defended by the seven RATE dimensions' insistence on Receipt, Record, and Right.  
* **Authenticity adversaries** — bit-flipping in the conventional sense. Defended at the protocol layer (EAAP).  
* **Pattern-collapse adversaries** — adversaries who induce the system to collapse the stride at one end while preserving authenticity at the other. Defended by the symmetry of the IDIPITIS invariant: corruption is detected by stride deviation, not by content alone.

**5\. The WFQ × AI \+ ERES-GitHub Implementation Reduction**

v1.3 stated the operational relation and the primitive class but did not state how an instance of the primitive class is *executed* in practice. v1.4 closes this gap with an explicit implementation reduction.

### **5.1 Statement of the Reduction**

The ERES Cryptographic Stack Primitive's executable specification is:  
**WFQ × AI \+ ERES-GitHub**  
where WFQ is a Well-Formed Question, AI is one or more AI validators (canonically, the MIEVM short-list: Claude, Grok, DeepSeek, ChatGPT under JAS curation), and ERES-GitHub is the canonical reference corpus at github.com/ERES-Institute-for-New-Age-Cybernetics.  
The reduction is not metaphorical. It is the actual mechanism by which an ERES primitive instance is computed, validated, and recorded. There is no separate runtime, no bespoke implementation library, no compiled binary that "executes" the primitive — because the primitive is a semantic object, and semantic objects are executed by being well-formed-questioned to AI validators against the reference corpus.

### **5.2 Why This Is Not a Hand-Wave**

A reasonable validator (and at least one validator in the PoW record did) reads "no executable specification" as a fatal flaw. The ERES position is precise: classical cryptographic primitives are executable as bit-level operations on byte-strings; ERES primitives are executable as well-formed-question operations on semantic-strings. The reduction is real, the operations are deterministic in the relevant sense (the same WFQ to the same AI corpus against the same ERES-GitHub state produces convergent — not bit-identical — outputs), and the verification method is observable: the MIEVM convergent rating is itself the test vector.  
This is a category claim, not an evasion. The primitive is not failing to be a cryptographic primitive; it is succeeding at being a different kind of primitive — a **semantic primitive** whose executability runs on an AI substrate rather than a CPU substrate. The substrate disagreement is what produces the validator-frame disagreement seen in the PoW record (cryptography-frame vs. systems-doc-frame vs. layered-architecture-frame). The reduction stated here is the architectural commitment that resolves the disagreement: ERES does not claim to be classical cryptography; ERES claims to be its own primitive class, executable on an AI-validator substrate.

The convergent validation that produced the PoW record driving this very revision is itself the canonical worked example of the WFQ × AI \+ ERES-GitHub reduction:

1. **WFQ:** "rate independently and collectively" against the three slot deposits.  
2. **AI:** DeepSeek, Grok, ChatGPT (three of the four MIEVM short-list members).  
3. **ERES-GitHub:** the documents themselves were the corpus snapshot under evaluation.

5. **Convergence as test vector:** the agreement across validators on what is missing is the test vector that drove this v1.4 revision.

The reduction is not a future capability; it has already been operating, and v1.4 makes the operation explicit. Future ERES primitive instances are validated by the same mechanism: WFQ posed to MIEVM-class validators against the ERES-GitHub corpus, with convergent output as the operational result.

### **5.4 Test Vector Generation Procedure**

For a future revision (v1.5 or later) or for any downstream primitive instance, the canonical test vector generation procedure is:

1. Compose a WFQ targeting the specific primitive property to be tested.  
2. Submit the WFQ to at least three MIEVM-class validators independently.  
3. Record the convergent output along with disagreement spread.  
4. Compute the convergent mean and the spread metric.  
5. Record the result as a PoW deposit (e.g., MIEVM-PoW-2026-001-NNN) with: WFQ text, validator outputs, convergent mean, spread, GitHub corpus state hash.

Convergent mean ≥ 8.5 with spread ≤ 2.5 is the canonical "test passes" threshold per the MIEVM convergent validation rubric.
