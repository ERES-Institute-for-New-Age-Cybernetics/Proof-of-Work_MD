### **Remaining weaknesses & realism constraints**

* **Scale still enormous** — $997B gross / \~$700–950B net annual is wartime-level commitment (\~3.4% of projected 2026 GDP around $28–30T). While WWII defense spending peaked at \~37–42% of GDP and total federal outlays hit higher, that was amid existential mobilization with rationing, conscription, and broad societal buy-in. Today's environment (projected debt already climbing toward 108–120% of GDP by 2030–2036 per CBO baselines) features deep polarization, lobbying power in energy/defense sectors, and skepticism toward new spending programs—even crisis-branded ones. The windfall tax \+ "fairness" framing helps, but passage via ARRES Act in months remains a high bar.  
* **Return participation still aspirational at scale** — The 1.25–1.5x multiplier and merit signaling are strong incentives, but translating \~30% charitable-household rate to sustained monthly Dividend returns during acute hardship is a stretch. Many in genuine need would keep the cash; higher earners might prefer direct tax strategies over this novel channel.  
* **GAIA adoption dynamics** — Even as an "automatic byproduct," building trust in a new citizen-owned digital node (especially internationally) during crisis requires flawless execution on privacy, interoperability, and zero-friction UX. Tech equity gaps, digital literacy barriers, and varying national data-sovereignty norms could slow Tier 2–3 rollout.  
* **Political & institutional hurdles** — Objective sunset triggers are well-designed on paper, but "emergency rescinded" often becomes politicized. International replication assumes willing sovereign adaptation without treaty-level coordination—possible in theory, but historically rare for fiscal-identity architectures.  
* **Evidence & modeling** — Better than v3.0 (SEC filings, charitable data, precedents), but still lacks full general-equilibrium modeling of bond-market reactions, currency effects, or cross-border coordination frictions.
