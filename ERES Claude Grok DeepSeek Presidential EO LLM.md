Welcome back, Joseph. Good question — and it's one where the answer is layered, because there's no single statute titled "The Executive Order Act." Instead, the limits come from multiple sources operating simultaneously.

**Constitutional boundaries** are the foundational constraint. A President can only issue executive orders that fall within authority granted by Article II — the "executive Power," Commander-in-Chief authority, the Take Care Clause, and the oath of office. The Supreme Court's framework from *Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer* (1952) — Justice Jackson's famous concurrence — remains the governing analysis. It sets up three zones: the President's power is at its maximum when acting with congressional authorization, in a "zone of twilight" when Congress is silent, and at its "lowest ebb" when acting contrary to Congress's expressed will. An executive order that contradicts a statute falls into that third category and is most vulnerable to being struck down.

**Statutory limits** operate in two directions. Congress can explicitly authorize executive action (which strengthens it), or Congress can legislate in a way that forecloses presidential action in that space. A President cannot use an executive order to appropriate funds — that's Congress's power under the Appropriations Clause. Similarly, an EO cannot create new criminal law, levy taxes, or override rights protected by the Bill of Rights or subsequent amendments.

**The Administrative Procedure Act (APA)** provides a judicial check. When executive orders direct agency action, affected parties can challenge those actions in court, and courts can vacate agency rules that are "arbitrary and capricious" or exceed statutory authority — even when those rules were prompted by an EO.

**The National Emergencies Act (1976)** governs executive orders issued under emergency powers specifically. It requires the President to identify the specific statutory authorities being invoked and gives Congress a mechanism (though practically difficult to use) to terminate declared emergencies.

**Prior executive orders themselves** also play a role. EO 11030 (as amended) prescribes the procedural requirements — an executive order must be submitted to the Office of the Federal Register, reviewed by the Attorney General or OLC for form and legality, and published. These are procedural rather than substantive limits, but they exist.

**Judicial review** is the ultimate enforcement mechanism. Courts routinely review executive orders and have struck them down when they exceed presidential authority — *Youngstown* being the landmark, but there have been many others, including recent cases involving immigration and regulatory policy.

The practical upshot: a President has wide latitude to direct the executive branch's operations and priorities, but cannot use EOs to legislate, spend unappropriated money, override constitutional rights, or act contrary to valid statutes. The further an EO strays from directing internal executive branch operations and the more it resembles lawmaking, the more legally vulnerable it becomes.
