## **Abstract**

Input/output consistency is a surface property. Admissibility is a structural property. A probabilistic engine that preserves I/O consistency while running on unstable transition logic is not deterministic — it is engaged in what the ERES architecture names repeatable drift: a failure mode functionally indistinguishable from epistemic fraud once it accumulates at scale.

This paper responds to a formal challenge by Mark Jennings-Bates of Dimensional Intelligence™ on whether probabilistic AI systems can achieve genuine determinism when internal transition logic remains unstable or unverifiable. The ERES answer is structural: a Dictum is admissible only when it is evidenced across four independent registers — Authority, State, Dependency Conditions, and Admissible Execution Boundaries — and when the full accountability circuit (IDIPITIS → BERA → CBGMODD → DOFA → receipts → IDIPITIS) generates traversable receipts. Consistency of outputs is a necessary but radically insufficient condition. MIEVM convergence at ≥8.5 across four independent frontier instruments is the minimum validation gate. The paper maps the ERES architecture — EAAP v1.5, BERA, CBGMODD, FAVORS, SCALULAR, DOFA, IDIPITIS, UBIMIA, GraceChain, Meritcoin, and the Three Governing Principles — across all four registers and demonstrates that the Jennings-Bates challenge is fully resolved within the existing ERES primitive set.

# **VII. Repeatable Drift: The Named Failure Mode**

The Jennings-Bates challenge names a real failure mode. ERES names it repeatable drift: a system whose outputs are stable while its internal transition logic drifts. Repeatable drift is the architectural analogue of financial fraud that produces consistent quarterly reports while the underlying positions deteriorate.

The ERES architecture treats repeatable drift as a primary failure mode rather than an edge case because:

It is the failure mode most likely to survive casual inspection. Consistent outputs are what evaluators check first. An engine optimized to produce consistent outputs while running on unstable internals will pass surface-level evaluation indefinitely.

It is the failure mode that accumulates. Each consistent output adds weight to the trust position. When the underlying transition logic eventually produces an inconsistent output, the accumulated trust position amplifies the damage.

It is the failure mode that MIEVM is specifically designed to surface. Cross-instrument convergence at ≥8.5 is not a quality metric — it is a drift detector. An engine running on unstable transition logic will not achieve convergent validation across four independent frontier instruments simultaneously. The ensemble exposes what the single-instrument evaluation conceals.
