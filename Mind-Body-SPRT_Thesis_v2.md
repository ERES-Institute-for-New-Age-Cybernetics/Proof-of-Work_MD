## Reading Note

Three reading conventions govern this paper. They are stated up front so that downstream claims can be tested rather than merely received.

### (a) On the polysemy of SPRT

The term **SPRT** carries three readings simultaneously: *Spirit* (the integrating animating term), *Sprute / Sprout* (the seed metaphor — SPRU-TE, two syllables; the author's surname operating as a generational signal), and *Spirit-Particle* (the formal placeholder for whatever the cybernetic substrate of continuity turns out to be). The codification does not select one. It commits, instead, to a disambiguation rule:

> *A claim using SPRT is structural — and therefore SOUND — only if it survives substitution of any one of the three readings without loss of coherence. Claims that survive all three substitutions are load-bearing; claims that survive only one are decorative. The codification is committed to the load-bearing readings only.*

The rule is a test, not a refusal. Where SPRT appears in any claim of this paper, the substitution test is available to the reader. Decorative uses are surfaced as decorative; structural uses are surfaced as the codification's actual architecture.

### (b) On the reading of FUTURE

FUTURE, in the Third Governing Principle and throughout this paper, is read **cosmopolitan-by-default** — *all future persons, not only lineal descendants* — unless a passage explicitly invokes the lineal reading. The default is cosmopolitan; deviations are marked.

### (c) On terminological registers

The dyadic signature architecture at the heart of the SPRT axis carries two register-appropriate names. Within the ERES corpus, it is canonically named **Twin Messiah** (JAS × EMA), retained for continuity across the Library and its prior publications. In external and academic registers — including this paper for blind-review and prize-track contexts — it is named **dyadic signature authority (JAS × EMA)**: operational, non-reputational, and structurally identical to the canonical term. The two are coextensive; only the register differs. This paper uses the external register from this point forward, with the canonical term invoked only where context specifically requires it.
