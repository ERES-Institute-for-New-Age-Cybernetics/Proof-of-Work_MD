## **CHAPTER 1 — THE AGE OF NOISE**

The 20th century ended the way most empires end: not with a sudden collapse, but with a slow and noisy confusion. The collapse was not always visible. The buildings stayed standing. The supermarkets remained lit. The televisions continued to speak. The elections continued to happen.

But the civilization that entered the 21st century had already suffered a fatal wound:

It had lost the ability to know itself.

In older ages, societies were primitive, but their ignorance was honest. A village knew when it was hungry. A tribe knew when its people were sick. A city knew when its water was poisoned. There was no illusion of certainty—only survival.

But modern civilization developed a new disease. It became advanced enough to hide its own suffering behind administrative abstraction. It could bury the poor inside spreadsheets. It could hide the dying inside hospitals. It could reduce human beings to statistics, and then reduce those statistics into arguments, and then reduce those arguments into entertainment.

Civilization entered an era of **Noise**.

The Noise was not merely loudness. It was not simply the presence of media. Noise was a deeper condition: the inability to distinguish truth from performance. Noise was the collapse of signal.

And when signal collapses, all decisions become guesswork.

What people called “freedom” often became the freedom to be misinformed. What people called “progress” became the progress of advertising. What people called “knowledge” became trivia.

It was a civilization full of screens, yet blind.

It was in this environment that the earliest seeds of the ERES Institute would appear, not as a public movement, but as an internal necessity: the need for **real feedback**.

Because no civilization survives without feedback.

That is not philosophy. That is cybernetics.

## **CHAPTER 2 — CIVILIZATION WITHOUT FEEDBACK**

Cybernetics, in its purest form, is the science of regulation. It is the mathematics of feedback loops. It is the architecture of systems that can correct themselves.

A thermostat is cybernetic.  
 A living cell is cybernetic.  
 A nervous system is cybernetic.  
 An ecosystem is cybernetic.

A civilization should be cybernetic.

But the modern world had become something else: a civilization that regulated itself using propaganda and delay. It did not respond to problems as they occurred. It responded when they became political.

The great tragedy was that the tools for feedback already existed.

The internet could have been a planetary nervous system.  
 Data could have been a healing instrument.  
 Technology could have been a measurement engine.

Instead, these tools became instruments of extraction.

Platforms were built not to measure reality, but to monetize attention. The human nervous system was turned into a commodity. People were not educated; they were trained into addiction.

The result was predictable:

* education became a credential industry  
* healthcare became a billing system  
* politics became a war of narratives  
* poverty became a permanent business model  
* homelessness became a feature of real estate economics  
* ecology became a marketing slogan

Civilization began to resemble a machine that had lost its sensors.

And a machine without sensors eventually destroys itself.

The early 21st century did not need a new ideology. It needed something older and more serious:

**a cybernetic restoration.**

But cybernetics itself had been forgotten. It was treated as a historical curiosity. It belonged to the mid-20th century, to Norbert Wiener, to control systems, to Cold War science.

Few recognized that cybernetics was not a field of history.

It was the operating principle of all life.

And the absence of cybernetics is not neutrality.

It is failure.

Instead, education became a factory for obedience.

The schools of the early 21st century often taught children to memorize, but not to measure. They taught compliance, but not system thinking. They taught standardized answers, but not standardized reality.

The student learned the illusion of success. The student learned to perform.

The collapse was slow. It did not arrive with sirens. It arrived with paperwork.

Teachers were overwhelmed. Administrators multiplied. Costs rose. Outcomes worsened. Degrees inflated. Debt exploded. The youth entered adulthood not as free citizens, but as indentured workers.

This was the first major evidence that civilization had lost its feedback loop.

When education fails, everything fails.

Because education is the training system for all future decision-makers.
